Gear system (not a shopping list)

The goal is not to buy everything. It is to build a layering, traction, and safety system that matches conditions. This page is ready for brand notes, model comparisons, and your own gear tiers.

Cost-effective rule: Spend money where failure is dangerous: warmth, traction, navigation, and emergency gear. Save money on convenience items until you know your actual needs.

Layering framework

  • Base layerMoisture management first. Avoid cotton and document your go-to fabric weights.
  • Active insulationWarmth while moving, with enough breathability to avoid sweat build-up.
  • ShellWind and snow protection, balanced against ventilation and mobility.
  • Stop layerPut it on immediately during breaks. This is your do-not-get-cold insurance.

Traction ladder

  • SpikesLow-angle ice and packed snow. Note the conditions where they become your default carry.
  • CramponsSteeper or firmer conditions. Track fit notes and boot compatibility.
  • Ice axeSteep snow travel and self-arrest practice. Pair this with actual training.
  • SnowshoesFlotation and trail breaking. Add local rules and when you consider them mandatory.

My gear tiers

  • Tier 1: Starter but safeThe minimum kit that covers most beginner winter hikes.
  • Tier 2: More objectivesUpgrades for longer routes, stronger wind, and colder stops.
  • Tier 3: Steep snow or winter climbingTechnical tools plus the training required to use them responsibly.
